Prambanan Hindu Temple - Historical Summary

Location: Prambanan is located in Central Java, Indonesia, approximately 20 kilometers east of Yogyakarta city on the border between Yogyakarta Special Region and Central Java Province. The temple complex sits at coordinates 7°39′16″S 110°25′48″E near Mount Merapi.

Historical Summary:
Prambanan Temple, also known as Rara Jonggrang or Shiva Granja, is one of the largest Hindu temples in Indonesia and a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1991. Built during the Mataram Kingdom era around the late 8th to early 9th century CE (approximately 750-856 AD), it was constructed under King Balitung Maha Sambu of the Sanjaya dynasty, with construction possibly continuing through several reigns until about 856 AD.

The temple complex covers approximately 4 square kilometers and consists of over 240 temples dedicated to Hindu deities—Vishnu as Brahma (the creator), Shiva as Vishnu (the destroyer), and others including Ganesha, Agni, Garuda, Hanoman, Lakshmi, Saraswati, Nandi, Durga, and Indra. The main temple is the tallest at 47 meters high with a square base of approximately 35 x 35 meters.

Prambanan was abandoned in the late 10th century when King Mpu Sindok moved his capital to East Java following volcanic eruptions from Mount Merapi and political shifts toward Buddhism under the Sailendra dynasty, who built Borobudur nearby. The temple remained largely unknown until its rediscovery by Dutch archaeologist Cornelis van der Burg on September 20, 1733, when he was clearing jungle vegetation around a tree that had grown over one of the temples.

The site underwent extensive restoration efforts beginning in the early 20th century and continuing through various phases to reconstruct collapsed structures using original stones (a technique called anastylosis). Today, Prambanan stands as Indonesia's most important Hindu temple complex alongside Borobudur Temple, forming a significant cultural landmark that attracts millions of visitors annually.
