Abstract:
This thesis explores the utilization of probabilistic synthetic data as a robust method for training AI models. By generating datasets that mimic real-world scenarios, this approach addresses challenges such as data scarcity and privacy concerns while enhancing model performance.


Introduction: Leveraging Probabilistic Synthetic Data in Training AI Models

Artificial intelligence has fundamentally transformed modern computational paradigms, with machine learning models serving as the cornerstone of this revolution. At the heart of every successful AI system lies a robust training process, wherein algorithms iteratively optimize their internal parameters by ingesting vast quantities of data. The quality, diversity, and representativeness of this training corpus directly dictate model performance, generalization capabilities, and real-world reliability. As neural architectures grow increasingly complex—from transformer-based language models to diffusion networks for generative tasks—the dependency on high-fidelity datasets has become more pronounced than ever. Consequently, the acquisition and curation of training data have emerged as critical bottlenecks in the AI development lifecycle.

Despite its paramount importance, securing authentic, large-scale datasets presents formidable obstacles. Real-world data collection is often prohibitively expensive, requiring extensive annotation efforts, specialized hardware, and prolonged deployment periods. More critically, privacy concerns and stringent regulatory frameworks—such as GDPR, HIPAA, and CCPA—severely restrict the collection, storage, and sharing of sensitive information, particularly in healthcare, finance, and autonomous systems. Additionally, real-world data frequently suffers from class imbalance, selection bias, and missing values, which can propagate harmful stereotypes or degrade model fairness. These constraints collectively stifle innovation, limit reproducibility, and raise ethical red flags, necessitating alternative data paradigms that preserve utility while mitigating risk.

In response to these challenges, synthetic data generation has emerged as a transformative approach to AI dataset curation. Synthetic data refers to artificially generated information that mimics the statistical properties and structural relationships of real-world datasets without containing any actual individual records. By leveraging computational models to simulate realistic scenarios, researchers and practitioners can produce scalable, privacy-preserving, and highly customizable training corpora. This paradigm shift has accelerated across industries, enabling continuous model development even in data-scarce or highly regulated environments. Among the various methodologies for synthetic data production, probabilistic approaches have gained prominence due to their mathematical rigor, flexibility, and ability to capture uncertainty inherent in natural systems.

Probabilistic synthetic data is constructed by modeling the underlying probability distributions of real-world variables, capturing not only central tendencies but also variance, covariance, and higher-order dependencies. The creation process typically begins with exploratory data analysis to identify key features and their joint distributions. Parametric or non-parametric probabilistic models—such as Gaussian mixture models, Bayesian networks, variational autoencoders, or generative adversarial networks—are then trained on anonymized or partially available real data. Once calibrated, these models sample new instances by drawing from the learned distribution, ensuring that generated records maintain statistical fidelity while eliminating direct linkage to original subjects. Rigorous validation protocols, including distributional alignment tests, utility assessments, and differential privacy guarantees, are subsequently applied to verify authenticity and safety.

The utilization of probabilistic synthetic data spans a wide array of applications across AI training pipelines. In supervised learning, it augments imbalanced datasets, enabling robust classification and regression models. In unsupervised and self-supervised regimes, it facilitates pre-training on domain-specific distributions without compromising confidentiality. Notably, in high-stakes fields like medical diagnostics, financial fraud detection, and autonomous vehicle simulation, probabilistic synthetic data allows developers to stress-test edge cases, simulate rare events, and iterate rapidly without ethical or legal repercussions. Furthermore, its inherent randomness supports robustness training, helping models generalize across noisy or adversarial conditions.

This thesis explores the theoretical foundations, methodological advancements, and practical implementations of leveraging probabilistic synthetic data in AI model training. It examines how distribution-aware generation techniques enhance model performance, mitigate bias, and comply with evolving data governance standards. By synthesizing recent literature, computational frameworks, and empirical case studies, this work establishes a comprehensive roadmap for integrating probabilistic synthetic datasets into modern machine learning workflows. Ultimately, it argues that probabilistic synthetic data is not merely a workaround for data scarcity but a foundational pillar for the next generation of ethical, scalable, and resilient AI systems.
